S26-04

Reignite the Mid-Semester Spark

EngagementClassroom Mgmt

Student motivation naturally dips as the semester progresses, but small changes can make a big impact.

Add Movement and Play

  • Offer a brief, structured break during longer class sessions
  • Try Team Jeopardy or quick minute-to-win-it challenges
  • Organize a walk-and-talk discussion to get students out of their seats

Increase Personal Connection

  • Invite a guest speaker to bring fresh energy and real-world insights
  • Use small-group peer discussions to break up lecture time

Create Flexibility

  • Survey students mid-semester: ask what's working and what's not
  • Consider adjusting workloads or deadlines for the rest of the semester
S26-03

Take Your Class Temperature

EngagementClassroom Mgmt

Mid-semester is the perfect time to check how students feel about your learning environment. Take 15–20 minutes for a direct think-pair-share:

  • Students form pairs, then groups of four, to discuss what's working and what could improve
  • Guide reflection: What's been most helpful? What's caused the most difficulty? What suggestions do you have?

This creates a productive class conversation, provides actionable feedback, and shows students you care about their journey.

S26-02

When Low-Tech Wins

Engagement

Printed group activities generate better collaboration than digital ones. Students talk, share ideas, and work together instead of scrolling on their screens.

  • Distribute one printed activity per group of ~4 students
  • Have students write their names on it for accountability

Pros: Faster to prepare, no device issues, forces face-to-face engagement.
Cons & solutions: Create a quick Catcourses quiz keyword for participation credit; one print per group minimizes waste.

S26-01

Welcome Students with Connection

Engagement

Before diving into course content, invest in introductions:

  • Introduce yourself: your love of teaching, your discipline, your hobbies, where you're from
  • Know your students: survey them on why they're here, what they hope to learn, their dream job
  • Help students meet each other: 5–10 minute icebreaker — bingo, two truths and a lie, blobs and lines…

These small investments show students you're excited to work with them this semester.

F25-15

Looking Back to Move Forward

AssessmentClassroom Mgmt

Finals week — the perfect time for a three-way reflection:

  • Students: extra-credit survey on what helped or limited their learning
  • TA(s): discuss what worked, gather suggestions
  • Yourself: evaluate which objectives were met, note successful activities

Key step: Pick ONE major change to implement next semester. Not five. One meaningful adjustment.

F25-14

Rethinking Finals in the AI Age

AssessmentAI & Tools

Browser extensions now read Catcourses quizzes and suggest answers in real-time. Any online or take-home assignment is potentially vulnerable. Consider:

  • Supervised pen-and-paper exams
  • Visual projects — infographics or posters requiring synthesis
  • Portfolio-based assessments demonstrating growth over time
  • Media projects — videos, podcasts, or presentations
  • Oral exams, individual or small-group
  • Assignments requiring personal experience, local data, or original analysis
  • If allowing AI: require students to document their process and critique AI outputs

F25-12

Build Study Skills, Not Study Guides

EngagementAI & Tools
  • Guide students to create their own: concept maps, Cornell notes, flashcards — make it an in-class activity
  • Point to existing resources: chapter objectives, end-of-lecture takeaway slides, weekly review quizzes
  • Build review into class time: one-minute papers, group concept mapping, Mentimeter/Wooclap sessions

F25-11

To Guide or Not to Guide?

Engagement

Every semester, students ask for study guides — a habit rooted in secondary education. The dilemma: should we give in?

Some say no: students need to develop their own study skills, and spoon-feeding creates passive learners. Others argue yes: dense college material can overwhelm even bright students, and structure helps while they're still developing skills.

What if there's a middle ground? See tip F25-12 for alternatives that actually work.

F25-10

Beat the Mid-Semester Slump

EngagementClassroom Mgmt
  • Acknowledge it: naming this period can be validating for students
  • Use media: play music as students enter or show a relevant clip
  • Flip your format: start with discussion, or try a "no-lecture day"
  • Connect to current events: news articles, podcasts, or videos linking concepts to the real world
  • Invite a guest: a colleague, alum, or grad student brings fresh perspective
  • Revisit your syllabus: consider adjustments based on current progress

Your energy impacts your students. A 10-minute walk before class can make all the difference.

F25-09

The Mid-Semester Check-In

EngagementAssessment

After submitting mid-semester grades, flip the script and get feedback on your course. Just 5–15 minutes can offer invaluable insights:

  • Quick online survey: Catcourses Quizzes, Qualtrics, Mentimeter, or Wooclap
  • Interactive discussion: 15-minute think-pair-share on what's working and what could improve
  • SATAL support: request a formal mid-semester evaluation through the Students Assessing Teaching and Learning program

Use feedback for small, actionable adjustments. This shows students you value their role in the classroom.

F25-08

It's Not Wrong, It's Learning

EngagementClassroom Mgmt

When a student's answer misses the mark, your response can shut down participation — or create a powerful learning moment:

  • Dig deeper: "Walk me through your thinking on that."
  • Build on it: "You're on the right track! Can someone add to that?"
  • Guide gently: "I like where you're going. Now consider this…"
  • Find the connection: link their response to a related concept, then circle back
  • Acknowledge the effort: recognize their willingness to try while explaining why the answer isn't quite right

F25-06

It's About the Ritual

Engagement

Opening rituals reduce anxiety, build positive energy, and signal that learning can be both rigorous and enjoyable:

  • Share a fun fact: a surprising tidbit related to today's topic
  • Learn about your students: "Starbucks or Dutch Bros?" — low effort, high connection
  • What's happening here?: project an intriguing image and ask students to describe what they see
  • Mindful breathing: 4-7-8 breath — inhale 4 counts, hold 7, exhale 8
  • Music: play something as students settle in

Consistency matters more than complexity. Find what feels authentic to you and stick with it.

F25-05

It's Not About the Joke

Engagement

Q: Who's the king of the classroom? A: The ruler.

I've been starting most classes with a dad joke for years. Students still request them and mention this ritual positively in course evaluations. What I've observed:

  • The classroom atmosphere becomes more relaxed and welcoming
  • Transitioning from social time to academic content feels smoother
  • Students engage more quickly with the instructor
  • Students approach me more readily during and after class

The specific activity matters less than the intention to create a welcoming space.

F25-04

The Power of the 2-Minute Break

EngagementClassroom Mgmt

Short breaks every 10–15 minutes boost student retention and give you a moment to recharge. Try any of these (2 mins each):

  • Review and Clarify: students review notes and jot down questions
  • Quick Quiz: Kahoot, Mentimeter, Wooclap, or clickers
  • Discussion: relate concepts to real-world examples or predict next lecture steps
  • Pair Share: partners explain key points, then share with the class
  • One-Minute Paper: write the most important point or lingering question
F25-03

Teaching in the TikTok News Era

EngagementClassroom Mgmt

A survey of 253 UC Merced students (ESS002 & ESS050) revealed that 78% use social media for daily news, with TikTok leading at 40%. Algorithms prioritize engagement over accuracy, limiting exposure to diverse perspectives.

  • Discuss source evaluation briefly in class
  • Lead a cross-referencing activity comparing different sources on the same topic
  • It's not about judging students' choices but building critical thinking skills
F25-02

Getting Students Back to Office Hours

EngagementClassroom Mgmt

Office hour attendance has declined significantly since COVID — research shows no recovery upon returning to in-person teaching. We need to actively promote them. Remind students that:

  • Office hours aren't just for struggling students — they're valuable for everyone
  • They improve understanding and grades through personalized clarification
  • They build connections for future recommendations and research opportunities

Mention office hours during lectures, send periodic reminders, and emphasize that asking questions shows engagement, not weakness.

Students Need AI-Aware Professors

AI & Tools

ChatGPT Plus is now free through May; Gemini Advanced free through finals 2026. These "gifts" are strategic investments in future customers. We must become AI-aware instructors.

  • Know what you're up against: spend 30 minutes weekly exploring AI tools to understand what students are using
  • Focus on process, not product: design assignments that value the learning journey
  • Trust your instincts: watch for sudden style shifts or references to concepts not covered in class
  • Be explicit: create clear AI policies
#12 · Spring '25

Catching the AI Wave Without Drowning

AI & ToolsAssessment

AI isn't coming to education — it's already here. How to respond:

  • Return to supervised assessments when possible
  • Design "AI-proof" assignments requiring personal experience or local data
  • Teach responsible AI use rather than ignoring its existence
  • Adjust what we value in student work (process over product)

Recommended reading: Teaching with AI: A Practical Guide to a New Era of Human Learning by José Bowen & Edward Watson (2024).

#08 · Spring '25

In AI Detectors, Trust You Won't

AI & ToolsAssessment

AI detection software has serious limitations: frequent false positives (especially for non-native speakers), easily fooled by "humanizer" tools, and questionable accuracy. Instead:

  • Design purposeful assignments: connect tasks clearly to learning outcomes
  • Focus on process: break assignments into stages that make student thinking visible
  • Trust your instincts: watch for sudden style shifts or generic content
#07 · Spring '25

A Picture Is Worth a Thousand Words

AI & Tools

Custom AI-generated visuals improve student understanding of complex concepts. Try: DALL-E (ChatGPT), Gemini, Microsoft Designer, or Adobe Firefly.

Example prompt: "Create a realistic photo of cumulus clouds forming by convection above a bare soil field surrounded by wetlands in California's Central Valley. Show water vapor rising from heated soil, distant mountains on the horizon."

Also: Napkin.ai transforms notes into polished visuals — ideal for illustrating concepts on the fly.

#06 · Spring '25

Let AI Do the Heavy Lifting in Assignment Creation

AI & ToolsAssessment
  • Brainstorming: "Create 2 quiz versions from the uploaded transcript — 6 multiple-choice, 3 true/false, 1 matching. Provide the key after each question."
  • Structuring: "Rewrite this assignment following the uploaded template. Limit learning objectives to two major ones."
  • Rubrics: "Create a 50-point rubric for a college-level Hydrology poster project — 5 criteria max, 3 grading categories."

Always treat AI output as a first draft that needs your expertise to refine.

#03 · Spring '25

Principal AI Assistants for Teaching

AI & Tools
  • ChatGPT: text and voice prompting, image and video creation (subscription)
  • Google Gemini: text-prompting with web-integrated responses and image creation
  • Microsoft Copilot: directly integrated in Microsoft Office tools
  • Claude: known for accuracy and academic-focused features (artifacts, projects, writing styles)

For academic work, I recommend Claude due to its precision, strong ethical framework, and robust capabilities for assessments, course materials, and detailed feedback. Always review and verify outputs before use.
